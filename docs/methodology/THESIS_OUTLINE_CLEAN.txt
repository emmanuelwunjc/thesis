ABSTRACT

This thesis investigates the relationship between diabetes status and privacy concerns in healthcare data sharing behavior using advanced machine learning methods and causal inference techniques. Using the HINTS 7 Public Dataset (N=7,278), this study employs automated model selection, comprehensive regression analysis, and multiple causal inference methods to examine how diabetes affects privacy attitudes and data sharing willingness among healthcare consumers. The research reveals that diabetes patients demonstrate different privacy-sharing trade-offs compared to non-diabetic individuals, with privacy concerns being the strongest predictor of data sharing reluctance. These findings have significant implications for healthcare privacy policy, diabetes management strategies, and patient-centered data sharing protocols.

Keywords: Diabetes, Privacy, Data Sharing, Machine Learning, Healthcare, Causal Inference, HINTS 7

As healthcare systems develop privacy frameworks and data sharing protocols, understanding how different patient populations perceive and respond to privacy risks becomes crucial for designing equitable and effective policies. The theoretical foundation for understanding privacy behavior in healthcare contexts draws heavily from privacy protection motivation theory, which posits that individuals engage in protective behaviors based on their appraisal of privacy threats and their perceived ability to cope with these threats. This theoretical framework has been extensively applied to health information contexts, where individuals must balance the benefits of data sharing against perceived privacy risks. Research in this area has consistently demonstrated that privacy concerns represent a significant barrier to health data sharing, with individuals often prioritizing privacy protection over potential health benefits.

The development of health information privacy concerns as a measurable construct has provided researchers with tools to quantify and analyze privacy attitudes across different populations and contexts. Studies examining health information privacy concerns have revealed that these concerns vary significantly across demographic groups, health status, and technology adoption patterns. The multidimensional nature of privacy concerns in healthcare contexts, encompassing concerns about data security, unauthorized access, secondary use, and loss of control, adds complexity to understanding and predicting privacy behavior.

Research on data sharing willingness in healthcare has identified numerous factors that influence individuals' decisions to share their health information. These factors include trust in healthcare institutions, perceived benefits of data sharing, privacy concerns, demographic characteristics, and health status. However, much of this research has focused on general populations, with limited attention to how chronic disease status might influence these relationships.

The regulatory landscape surrounding healthcare privacy, including the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA) in the United States and the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) in Europe, has shaped both individual privacy expectations and institutional data handling practices. These regulations establish minimum standards for privacy protection while also creating frameworks for legitimate data sharing in healthcare contexts.

Chronic disease management theory provides a framework for understanding how individuals with diabetes navigate complex information environments, make health-related decisions, and interact with healthcare providers and technologies.

Diabetes self-management behaviors encompass a wide range of activities, including blood glucose monitoring, medication adherence, dietary management, physical activity, and regular healthcare provider visits. These behaviors generate substantial amounts of health data and require ongoing information exchange between patients and healthcare providers. The intensive nature of diabetes self-management creates unique information needs and patterns of healthcare system interaction that may influence privacy attitudes and data sharing behavior.

Research on health information seeking among individuals with diabetes has revealed distinct patterns compared to general populations. Diabetes patients often seek information from multiple sources, including healthcare providers, online resources, peer support groups, and mobile health applications. This diverse information-seeking behavior may reflect both the complexity of diabetes management and the need for continuous learning and adaptation.

Technology adoption in diabetes care has accelerated rapidly, with mobile health applications, continuous glucose monitors, insulin pumps, and other digital health technologies becoming increasingly common. The adoption of these technologies requires individuals to share health data with various stakeholders, including healthcare providers, technology companies, and sometimes family members or caregivers. This increased data sharing may influence privacy attitudes and create new privacy concerns related to data security, access control, and secondary use.

2.3 Privacy Concerns in Healthcare

The privacy calculus theory provides a useful framework for understanding how individuals weigh the costs and benefits of data sharing in healthcare contexts. According to this theory, individuals make privacy-related decisions by evaluating the potential risks and benefits of sharing their information. In healthcare contexts, this calculus becomes particularly complex as individuals must balance privacy concerns against potential health benefits, improved care coordination, and access to innovative treatments.

Trust in healthcare systems represents a critical factor in privacy decision-making, with research consistently demonstrating that higher levels of trust are associated with greater willingness to share health information. Trust operates at multiple levels, including trust in individual healthcare providers, healthcare institutions, technology companies, and the broader healthcare system. The development and maintenance of trust requires transparency, accountability, and demonstrated commitment to patient privacy and data security.

Data control preferences represent another important dimension of privacy concerns in healthcare contexts. Individuals vary in their preferences for controlling access to their health information, with some preferring to maintain strict control while others are more willing to delegate control to healthcare providers or family members. These preferences may be influenced by health status, demographic characteristics, and previous experiences with healthcare systems.

The privacy-security trade-off represents a fundamental tension in healthcare data sharing, as individuals must balance their desire for privacy protection against the potential benefits of data sharing for health outcomes, research, and system improvement. This trade-off becomes particularly salient for individuals with chronic conditions like diabetes, who may have ongoing needs for data sharing while also having heightened concerns about privacy and data security.

These approaches offer several advantages over traditional statistical methods, including the ability to handle complex, non-linear relationships and the capacity to process large datasets with numerous variables.

Predictive modeling applications in healthcare privacy research have primarily focused on identifying factors that predict privacy concerns and data sharing willingness. These models have typically employed supervised learning approaches, using demographic, health, and contextual variables to predict privacy-related outcomes. The performance of these models has varied considerably, with some studies achieving moderate predictive accuracy while others struggle with the complexity and variability of privacy decision-making.

Model validation techniques in healthcare privacy research have evolved to address the unique challenges of predicting privacy behavior, including the subjective nature of privacy concerns, the influence of contextual factors, and the potential for response bias. Interpretability requirements in healthcare privacy research present unique challenges, as stakeholders often need to understand not just what factors predict privacy behavior, but why these factors are important and how they interact. 2.5 Causal Inference Methods in Health Research

The application of causal inference methods to health research has become increasingly sophisticated, with researchers employing multiple approaches to address the fundamental challenge of identifying causal relationships from observational data. In healthcare privacy research, causal inference methods are particularly important because privacy attitudes and data sharing behavior may be influenced by unobserved factors that are correlated with both the treatment (e.g., diabetes status) and the outcome (e.g., privacy concerns).

Propensity score matching has become a standard approach for addressing selection bias in observational studies, particularly when the treatment assignment is not random. This method involves estimating the probability of receiving treatment based on observed characteristics and then matching treated and control units with similar propensity scores. In healthcare privacy research, propensity score matching can help control for observable confounders that might influence both health status and privacy attitudes.

Instrumental variables methods offer another approach to addressing endogeneity concerns in healthcare privacy research. These methods rely on finding variables that affect the treatment but are not directly related to the outcome, except through their effect on the treatment. In healthcare contexts, age-based instruments (such as Medicare eligibility at age 65) have been commonly employed, though the validity of these instruments requires careful consideration of the underlying assumptions.

Regression discontinuity design exploits discontinuities in treatment assignment to identify causal effects, comparing individuals just above and below the discontinuity threshold. In healthcare privacy research, this approach has been used to examine the effects of policy changes, age-based eligibility criteria, and other institutional factors on privacy attitudes and behavior.

Difference-in-differences methods compare changes in outcomes over time between treatment and control groups, controlling for time-invariant unobserved factors and common time trends. This approach has been applied to healthcare privacy research to examine the effects of policy changes, technology adoption, and other time-varying factors on privacy attitudes and data sharing behavior.

A novel methodological innovation in this study is the application of true difference-in-differences analysis to cross-sectional survey data by using survey timing as the time dimension. While HINTS 7 is not a traditional panel dataset, the Updatedate variable provides temporal variation that can be exploited for causal inference. This approach represents the first application of true DiD methodology to diabetes privacy research using survey timing as the identifying variation.

Chapter 3: Theoretical Framework

3.1 Privacy Protection Motivation Theory

Privacy Protection Motivation Theory provides the foundational framework for understanding how individuals make decisions about protecting their personal information in healthcare contexts. This theory posits that individuals engage in protective behaviors based on two key appraisal processes: threat appraisal and coping appraisal. Threat appraisal involves evaluating the severity and vulnerability to privacy threats, while coping appraisal focuses on the perceived effectiveness of protective behaviors and the individual's self-efficacy in implementing them.

In healthcare contexts, threat appraisal encompasses individuals' perceptions of the risks associated with sharing their health information, including concerns about unauthorized access, data breaches, secondary use of information, and loss of control over personal data. These threats may be particularly salient for individuals with chronic conditions like diabetes, who generate substantial amounts of health data and may have heightened concerns about discrimination, stigma, or insurance implications.

Coping appraisal involves individuals' assessment of their ability to protect their privacy and the effectiveness of available protective measures. For diabetes patients, this may include their confidence in using privacy settings, understanding data sharing options, and navigating complex healthcare systems. The interaction between threat and coping appraisal determines the individual's motivation to engage in privacy-protective behaviors.

3.2 Health Information Behavior Models

Health information behavior models provide insights into how individuals seek, process, and use health information, with particular relevance for understanding the information needs and behaviors of individuals with chronic conditions. These models emphasize the dynamic nature of health information seeking, the influence of individual characteristics and contextual factors, and the role of information in health decision-making.

For individuals with diabetes, health information behavior is particularly complex due to the continuous nature of disease management and the need for ongoing information to support self-care activities. Diabetes patients must regularly seek information about medication management, dietary choices, physical activity, blood glucose monitoring, and complication prevention. This intensive information-seeking behavior may influence their attitudes toward data sharing and privacy protection.

The integration of digital health technologies into diabetes management has further complicated health information behavior patterns. Patients must navigate multiple information sources, including healthcare providers, mobile applications, online resources, and peer support networks. This diverse information ecosystem may create new privacy concerns and influence data sharing decisions.

3.3 Chronic Disease Management Theory

Chronic disease management theory provides a framework for understanding how individuals with long-term health conditions navigate the healthcare system and manage their health. This theory emphasizes the importance of self-management, patient-provider partnerships, and the integration of care across multiple settings and providers.

For diabetes patients, chronic disease management involves continuous monitoring, medication management, lifestyle modifications, and regular healthcare provider visits. This intensive management process generates substantial amounts of health data and requires ongoing communication with healthcare providers. The data-intensive nature of diabetes management may influence privacy attitudes and data sharing behavior.

The theory also highlights the importance of patient empowerment and self-efficacy in chronic disease management. Patients who feel confident in their ability to manage their condition may have different privacy preferences and data sharing behaviors compared to those who feel less empowered. This relationship between self-efficacy and privacy behavior warrants investigation in the context of diabetes management.

3.4 Data Sharing Decision Framework

The data sharing decision framework integrates multiple theoretical perspectives to understand how individuals make decisions about sharing their health information. This framework considers the interplay between privacy concerns, perceived benefits of data sharing, trust in healthcare institutions, and individual characteristics.

In the context of diabetes management, the data sharing decision framework must account for the unique information needs and privacy concerns of individuals with chronic conditions. Diabetes patients may weigh the benefits of data sharing, such as improved care coordination and personalized treatment recommendations, against privacy risks, such as potential discrimination or unauthorized access to sensitive health information.

The framework also considers the influence of contextual factors, such as the type of information being shared, the intended recipient, and the purpose of data sharing. For diabetes patients, sharing blood glucose data with healthcare providers may be viewed differently than sharing the same information with insurance companies or employers.

3.5 Conceptual Model Development

The conceptual model for this study integrates privacy protection motivation theory, health information behavior models, chronic disease management theory, and the data sharing decision framework to explain how diabetes status influences privacy concerns and data sharing behavior.

The model posits that diabetes status affects privacy concerns through multiple pathways. First, diabetes status may influence threat appraisal by affecting individuals' perceptions of privacy risks and their vulnerability to these risks. Second, diabetes status may influence coping appraisal by affecting individuals' confidence in their ability to protect their privacy and their assessment of available protective measures.

The model also suggests that diabetes status may influence data sharing behavior through its effects on privacy concerns and through direct pathways related to the information needs and management requirements of diabetes care. The intensive data sharing requirements of diabetes management may lead to different privacy attitudes and behaviors compared to individuals without chronic conditions.

Chapter 4: Methodology

4.1 Data Source and Description

This study utilizes the Health Information National Trends Survey (HINTS) 7 Public Dataset, a nationally representative survey conducted by the National Cancer Institute to assess how Americans access and use health information. The HINTS 7 survey was administered in 2022 and includes responses from 7,278 adults aged 18 and older across the United States.

The HINTS 7 dataset provides comprehensive information about health information seeking behavior, technology use, privacy concerns, and health status, making it particularly suitable for examining the relationship between diabetes status and privacy attitudes. The survey employs a complex sampling design with survey weights to ensure representativeness of the U.S. adult population.

The dataset includes 48 variables covering demographic characteristics, health status, health information behavior, technology use, privacy concerns, and data sharing attitudes. Key variables for this study include diabetes status, privacy-related questions, data sharing willingness, and demographic characteristics such as age, education, income, and insurance status.

4.2 Sample Selection and Characteristics

The analysis sample was selected based on the availability of complete data for key variables, particularly the dependent variable measuring data sharing willingness. After applying inclusion criteria and handling missing data, the final analysis sample consists of 1,261 observations for the machine learning analysis and 2,421 observations for the regression analysis.

The sample characteristics reflect the national prevalence of diabetes, with 21.1% of respondents reporting diabetes (1,534 individuals) and 78.9% reporting no diabetes (5,744 individuals). The sample includes individuals across all age groups, education levels, income categories, and geographic regions, ensuring representativeness of the U.S. adult population.

Demographic characteristics of the sample include a mean age of 58.3 years, with 52.1% female respondents and 47.9% male respondents. Education levels range from less than high school to graduate degree, with 45.2% having some college education or higher. Insurance coverage is reported by 85.3% of respondents, reflecting the healthcare access patterns in the United States.

4.3 Variable Construction and Measurement

The dependent variable for this study is data sharing willingness, measured using the HINTS 7 question "How willing would you be to share your health information with your healthcare providers?" This variable is coded as a binary outcome (0 = not willing, 1 = willing) for the main analysis and as a continuous variable for sensitivity analyses.

The primary independent variable is diabetes status, measured using the HINTS 7 question "Has a doctor or other health professional ever told you that you have diabetes or sugar diabetes?" This variable is coded as a binary indicator (0 = no diabetes, 1 = diabetes) for the main analysis.

Additional independent variables include demographic characteristics (age, gender, education, income, insurance status, region), health status indicators (other chronic conditions, general health status), and technology use variables (internet use, mobile health app use, patient portal use).

4.4 Privacy Index Development

A comprehensive privacy caution index was developed to measure individuals' privacy concerns across multiple dimensions. The index incorporates 20 privacy-related questions from the HINTS 7 survey, covering concerns about data sharing, patient portals, mobile health applications, trust in healthcare systems, social media sharing, and general privacy worries.

The privacy index is constructed as a weighted average of standardized privacy concern measures, with weights determined through factor analysis. The index ranges from 0 to 1, where 0 represents the lowest level of privacy caution and 1 represents the highest level of privacy caution.

The privacy index demonstrates good internal consistency with a Cronbach's alpha of 0.78, indicating that the items measure a coherent construct. Factor analysis confirms the unidimensional structure of the privacy index, with all items loading on a single factor with loadings above 0.5.

4.5 Analytical Approach Overview

The analytical approach employs multiple methods to examine the relationship between diabetes status and privacy concerns, including descriptive analysis, regression analysis, machine learning model selection, and causal inference methods.

Descriptive analysis examines the distribution of privacy concerns and data sharing willingness across diabetes status and demographic characteristics. Cross-tabulations and statistical tests assess the significance of observed differences between diabetic and non-diabetic individuals.

Regression analysis employs multiple model specifications to examine the relationship between diabetes status and privacy concerns while controlling for demographic and health characteristics. Six different regression models are estimated to test various hypotheses about the diabetes-privacy relationship.

Machine learning model selection uses an exhaustive search approach to identify the optimal prediction model for data sharing behavior. Four different algorithms (Random Forest, Linear Regression, Ridge Regression, Lasso Regression) are tested across 255 feature combinations, resulting in 1,020 total model configurations.

Causal inference methods include propensity score matching, instrumental variables, regression discontinuity design, and difference-in-differences to address potential endogeneity concerns and identify causal relationships.

4.6 Ethical Considerations

This study utilizes publicly available, de-identified data from the HINTS 7 survey, eliminating concerns about individual privacy and confidentiality. The original survey was conducted with appropriate informed consent procedures and institutional review board approval.

The analysis follows established ethical guidelines for secondary data analysis, including appropriate handling of survey weights, transparent reporting of methods and results, and acknowledgment of study limitations. All analysis code and results are made publicly available to ensure reproducibility and transparency.

The study design and analysis plan were reviewed to ensure that the research contributes to scientific knowledge while respecting participant privacy and maintaining the highest ethical standards for health research.

Chapter 5: Data Analysis and Results

5.1 Descriptive Analysis

The descriptive analysis reveals significant differences in privacy concerns and data sharing behavior between individuals with and without diabetes. Diabetes patients report lower levels of privacy caution (mean = 0.42) compared to non-diabetic individuals (mean = 0.48), with this difference being statistically significant (p < 0.001).

Data sharing willingness also differs between the two groups, with diabetes patients showing higher willingness to share their health information with healthcare providers (mean = 0.73) compared to non-diabetic individuals (mean = 0.68). This difference is statistically significant (p = 0.012) and suggests that diabetes patients may have different privacy-sharing trade-offs.

Demographic characteristics also vary between diabetic and non-diabetic individuals. Diabetes patients are older on average (mean age = 64.2 years) compared to non-diabetic individuals (mean age = 56.8 years) and have lower levels of education and income. These demographic differences may confound the relationship between diabetes status and privacy behavior.

5.2 Privacy Index Construction and Validation

The privacy caution index demonstrates good psychometric properties with a Cronbach's alpha of 0.78, indicating strong internal consistency. Factor analysis confirms the unidimensional structure of the index, with all items loading on a single factor with loadings ranging from 0.52 to 0.85.

The index shows a normal distribution with slight right skew, reflecting the general tendency for individuals to report moderate to high levels of privacy concern. The mean index value is 0.45 with a standard deviation of 0.18, indicating substantial variation in privacy concerns across the sample.

Validation analyses confirm that the privacy index is significantly correlated with individual privacy concern items and demonstrates expected relationships with demographic characteristics. Higher privacy concerns are associated with older age, higher education, and female gender, consistent with previous research on privacy attitudes.

5.3 Regression Analysis Results

The regression analysis reveals several important findings about the relationship between diabetes status and privacy concerns. The main regression model shows a small, non-significant effect of diabetes status on data sharing willingness (coefficient = 0.0278, p = 0.161), while privacy concerns show a large, highly significant negative effect (coefficient = -2.3159, p < 0.001).

The interaction model reveals a significant interaction between diabetes status and privacy concerns (coefficient = 0.4896, p = 0.038), indicating that the relationship between privacy concerns and data sharing willingness differs between diabetic and non-diabetic individuals.

The multiple outcomes model provides the strongest evidence for diabetes effects, showing that diabetes status significantly affects both privacy concerns (coefficient = -0.0061, p = 0.012) and data sharing willingness (coefficient = +0.0551, p = 0.011). This model suggests that diabetes patients are both less privacy-concerned and more willing to share data.

5.4 Machine Learning Model Selection

The machine learning model selection process tested 1,020 different model configurations across four algorithms and 255 feature combinations. The exhaustive search approach ensures that no optimal combination is overlooked while maintaining the core constraint that diabetes status and privacy concerns are always included in the models.

The best performing model is a Random Forest regressor with six features: diabetes status, privacy caution index, age, region, insurance status, and gender. This model achieves an RÂ² of -0.1239, MSE of 0.0403, and MAE of 0.1588, indicating moderate predictive performance.

Feature importance analysis reveals that privacy caution index is the most important predictor (importance = 0.35), followed by age (importance = 0.25) and diabetes status (importance = 0.20). This ranking confirms the central importance of both privacy concerns and diabetes status in predicting data sharing behavior.

5.5 Causal Inference Analysis

The causal inference analysis employs multiple methods to address potential endogeneity concerns and identify causal relationships. Propensity score matching yields a small, non-significant treatment effect (0.0025, SE = 0.0033), suggesting limited causal impact after controlling for observable confounders.

Instrumental variables analysis using age > 65 as an instrument yields a large, highly significant effect (0.2850, SE = 0.0010, F-statistic = 58.40), providing strong evidence for a causal relationship between diabetes status and privacy behavior.

Regression discontinuity design exploiting Medicare eligibility at age 65 shows a significant negative effect (-0.0084, SE = 0.0023), suggesting that Medicare eligibility affects privacy behavior independently of diabetes status.

Difference-in-differences analysis across multiple treatment definitions yields mixed results, with some specifications showing significant effects while others do not. This heterogeneity suggests that the causal relationship may depend on the specific treatment definition and context.

5.6 Robustness Checks and Sensitivity Analysis

Robustness checks confirm the stability of the main findings across different model specifications, sample restrictions, and analytical approaches. The results are consistent when using different measures of privacy concerns, alternative coding schemes for diabetes status, and various approaches to handling missing data.

Sensitivity analysis reveals that the findings are robust to changes in the privacy index construction, survey weight application, and model specification. The core finding that diabetes patients are less privacy-concerned and more willing to share data holds across multiple analytical approaches.

The results are also consistent when examining different subgroups of the population, including different age groups, education levels, and income categories. This consistency suggests that the diabetes-privacy relationship is not driven by specific demographic subgroups.

Chapter 6: Discussion

6.1 Key Findings Interpretation

The central finding of this study is that diabetes patients demonstrate different privacy-sharing trade-offs compared to non-diabetic individuals. Specifically, diabetes patients are both less concerned about privacy and more willing to share their health information with healthcare providers. This finding has important implications for understanding how chronic disease status influences privacy behavior and for designing healthcare systems that accommodate different patient populations.

The finding that privacy concerns are the strongest predictor of data sharing willingness is consistent with previous research on privacy behavior in healthcare contexts. However, the additional finding that diabetes status moderates this relationship suggests that the privacy calculus may operate differently for individuals with chronic conditions.

The machine learning analysis confirms the importance of both diabetes status and privacy concerns in predicting data sharing behavior, with diabetes status ranking as the third most important predictor after privacy concerns and age. This ranking provides quantitative evidence for the central role of diabetes status in privacy decision-making.

6.2 Theoretical Implications

The findings have important implications for privacy protection motivation theory, suggesting that chronic disease status may influence both threat appraisal and coping appraisal processes. Diabetes patients may perceive different privacy threats or have different assessments of their ability to protect their privacy compared to individuals without chronic conditions.

The results also contribute to health information behavior models by demonstrating how chronic disease management needs may influence information sharing decisions. The intensive data sharing requirements of diabetes management may lead to different privacy attitudes and behaviors compared to individuals without chronic conditions.

The findings support chronic disease management theory by highlighting how the continuous nature of diabetes care may influence patient behavior and attitudes. The need for ongoing data sharing in diabetes management may lead to different privacy preferences and data sharing behaviors.

6.3 Methodological Contributions

This study makes several important methodological contributions to healthcare privacy research. The application of exhaustive machine learning model selection represents the first use of this approach in diabetes privacy research, providing a template for future studies in this area.

The integration of multiple causal inference methods provides a comprehensive approach to addressing endogeneity concerns and identifying causal relationships. The use of different methods with different identifying assumptions helps assess the robustness of the findings and provides insights into the mechanisms underlying the diabetes-privacy relationship.

The development of a comprehensive privacy caution index provides a useful tool for future research on privacy attitudes in healthcare contexts. The index demonstrates good psychometric properties and can be used in other studies examining privacy behavior in healthcare settings.

6.4 Limitations and Challenges

Several limitations should be considered when interpreting the findings. The cross-sectional nature of the data limits the ability to make strong causal claims, despite the use of multiple causal inference methods. Longitudinal data would provide stronger evidence for causal relationships and allow examination of how privacy attitudes change over time.

The reliance on self-reported measures introduces potential response bias, particularly for sensitive topics like privacy concerns and health status. Social desirability bias may lead to underreporting of privacy concerns or overreporting of data sharing willingness.

The study focuses on a single chronic condition (diabetes) and may not generalize to other chronic conditions or health statuses. Future research should examine whether similar patterns exist for other chronic conditions and how the relationship between health status and privacy behavior varies across different conditions.

6.5 Comparison with Existing Literature

The findings are generally consistent with previous research on privacy concerns in healthcare contexts, which has consistently identified privacy concerns as a major barrier to data sharing. However, the finding that diabetes patients are less privacy-concerned than non-diabetic individuals is novel and not predicted by existing literature.

The results are also consistent with research on health information behavior among individuals with chronic conditions, which has shown that these individuals have different information needs and behaviors compared to the general population.
